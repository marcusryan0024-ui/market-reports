Strat Combos
▲ Bullish
2-1-2 Bullish Continuation
2↑ → 1 (inside coil) → 2↑ same direction. Most common setup. Wait for the inside bar to trigger before entry.
2-1-2 Bullish Reversal
2↓ → 1 (coil) → 2↑ opposite direction. Reversal off a key level. Needs higher TF FTFC for conviction.
3-2-2 Bullish
Outside bar (range expansion) → two directional bars same direction. Powerful continuation. Enter on the second 2 trigger.
1-2-2 Bullish
Inside bar (compression) → two directional bars same direction. Classic breakout from a coil. Volume should expand on the break.
2-2 Bullish Reversal
2↓ → 2↑ back the other way. Counter-trend. Strongest when the first 2 is also a Failed bar on a higher TF.
Rev Strat Bullish (3-2U)
Outside bar at key S/R → 2↑ takes out the high. Enter on the break. High conviction reversal signal.
Bullish Kicker
Bear close → gap up → bull open with no body overlap. Institutional demand. Highest conviction reversal in The Strat.
▼ Bearish
2-1-2 Bearish Continuation
2↓ → 1 (inside coil) → 2↓ same direction. Distribution into a coil. Wait for the inside bar low to break before entry.
2-1-2 Bearish Reversal
2↑ → 1 (coil) → 2↓ opposite direction. Reversal at a key level. Needs higher TF FTFC pointing down for conviction.
3-2-2 Bearish
Outside bar (range expansion) → two directional bars to the downside. Enter on the second 2↓ trigger. Flush continuation.
1-2-2 Bearish
Inside bar (compression) → two directional bars to the downside. Breakdown from coil. Volume should expand on the break.
2-2 Bearish Reversal
2↑ → 2↓ back the other way. Counter-trend short. Strongest when the 2↑ is a Failed bar on a higher TF.
Rev Strat Bearish (3-2D)
Outside bar at key S/R → 2↓ takes out the low. Enter on the break. High conviction reversal signal to the downside.
Bearish Kicker
Bull close → gap down → bear open with no body overlap. Institutional supply. Highest conviction bearish reversal in The Strat.
Candle Types (The Strat)
1
Inside Bar
Price coiling inside the prior bar. Market is undecided — sit on your hands until one side breaks.
2↑
Directional Up
Takes out prior High, NOT prior Low. Pure bullish pressure. Trigger = break of this bar’s high on the next bar.
2↓
Directional Down
Takes out prior Low, NOT prior High. Pure bearish pressure. Trigger = break of this bar’s low on the next bar.
3
Outside Bar
Takes out BOTH prior High and prior Low. Full range expansion. No directional edge — wait for the next bar to commit to a side.
F2U
Failed 2 Up
Exceeds prior High then reverses and closes back inside. Bulls trapped. Failed high = resistance. Bearish reversal signal.
F2D
Failed 2 Down
Exceeds prior Low then reverses and closes back inside. Bears trapped. Failed low = support. Bullish reversal signal.
BK
Bullish Kicker
Bear close → gap up → bull open with NO body overlap. Institutional demand. Highest-conviction reversal in The Strat.
Basics Options Trading
▼
Call Option▼
RIGHT (not obligation) to BUY shares at agreed upon price, on or before expiration
Allows you to buy at the strike price anytime before expiration — no matter where stock trades
Exercise = take advantage of the right to buy at a lower price
Put Option▼
RIGHT (not obligation) to SELL shares at agreed upon price, on or before expiration
Allows you to SELL at the strike price anytime before expiration — no matter where stock trades
Buying Put = Buying Insurance | Selling Put = Selling Insurance
Strike Price & Expiration▼
Strike Price = the agreed upon price at which stock is bought or sold
Expiration Date = the date the contract expires
Example: Call | Strike $120 | Exp 30 days → can buy at $120 anytime within 30 days
Why Trade Options?▼
Options = Insurance for an investor — you don't have to own the stock
Less Capital → Less Risk
Reading the Option Chain▼
Option Chain = list of all available options & the price of each one
Bid Price = SELL (where market is willing to buy FROM you)
Ask Price = BUY (where the sellers are)
1 Contract = 100 shares — remove the decimal to get dollar cost
Cost = Ask Price × 100 | Example: $2.81 × 100 = $281 premium
P&L Math▼
1 Contract: Buy @ $2.81 ($281) → Sell @ $4.00 ($400) = +$119 profit
2 Contracts: Buy @ $2.81 ($562) → Sell @ $4.00 ($800) = +$238 profit
3 Factors That Move Option Prices▼
1. Price of the Underlying Stock
2. Time Till Expiration
3. Volatility of the Underlying Stock
Call price goes UP when stock goes UP | Put price goes UP when stock goes DOWN
ITM vs OTM▼
ITM = allows purchase at a DISCOUNT to market price — further ITM = more expensive
OTM = no discount advantage — further OTM = cheaper, lower chance of becoming ITM
At expiration: ITM = has intrinsic value | OTM = WORTHLESS
Intrinsic Value = the ITM portion of an option's price (difference between stock & strike)
Time Decay & Extrinsic Value▼
More time = more expensive | Less time = cheaper (same as car insurance)
Extrinsic Value = amount option price is GREATER than intrinsic value
NO TIME LEFT = NO TIME VALUE — extrinsic decays to zero
Time Decay is AGAINST you as a buyer — you are fighting it every single day
Implied Volatility (IV)▼
High Volatility = large price swings = more expensive options
Low Volatility = small price swings = cheaper options
IV = expected volatility range — like a FEAR gauge for the market
High IV = Expensive options | Low IV = Cheap options
The Greeks — Overview▼
Measures of how option price reacts to: stock price moves, time, and IV
Helps choose strike selection & expiration
4 Main Greeks: Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega
Delta (Δ)▼
How much option price changes per $1 move in the stock
Example: Delta 0.65 → stock up $1.00 → option up $0.65
Not fixed — changes as stock moves (driven by Gamma)
Further ITM = higher delta | Further OTM = lower delta
Negative delta = SHORT (bearish) | Positive delta = LONG (bullish)
Bigger delta = more directional exposure — subjective to your risk tolerance
Gamma (Γ)▼
Amount by which DELTA changes per $1 move in the stock
As option goes deeper ITM, delta increases — Gamma drives that change
GAMMA NOT THAT IMPORTANT — don't overfocus on it
Theta (Θ) — Time Decay▼
Amount option price changes per 1 day passing
Works AGAINST buyers, FOR sellers — time value constantly being removed
Vega (V)▼
Amount option price changes per 1% change in IV
High IV = more expensive | Low IV = cheaper

What Heatseeker Is (& Is Not)▼
Visual map of dealer exposure — shows where price is pulled, pinned, stalled, or explosively moves
Heatseeker = context engine (dealer positioning context). Price action = trigger. You = risk manager
Shows WHERE price is likely to react — NOT WHEN. Shows where pressure exists, not what price must do
NOT: a signal system · a prediction engine · a standalone strategy · a guaranteed profit tool
Markets aren't random — dealers hedge → hedging moves price. Follow dealer pressure, not noise
Market Participants: Dealers (MMs) = liquidity providers, hedge risk, move price | Institutions = large, slow, anchor levels | Customers (retail) = chaotic flow that forces dealer hedging
Node Types & Color Logic▼
Each strike = node w/ value + color (absolute value determines color). Higher value = stronger pull
Green → Yellow → Purple = higher volume. Blue → Purple = higher volume
King Nodes: highest value nodes. Where price often gravitates. Late day → pin. Early touch → reject/drive away
Purple Nodes: volatility amplifier. Fast spikes & dumps. Often sharp reversals
Yellow Nodes: volatility dampener. Price slows/pins. Good for reversals & range trades
Gatekeeper Nodes: act as barriers before King Nodes. Test + fail → map reshuffle. Often mark early reversals
Hedge Nodes: appear during macro/news. Far from price. Slow protective positioning. Closer = more influence
★ Color does NOT define importance — MAGNITUDE DOES ★
Magnet Physics & Freshness Rule▼
Far from node = weak pull. Near node = strong pull. At node = stall/bounce/reject/pin
Think ZONES, not exact lines
Nodes attract & repel — trending day: price flows toward big node (momentum works) | Range day: between yellow nodes (expect expansion) | Breakout day: fast entry into purple (fade edges)
Freshness Rule: 1st touch = strongest/best. 2nd touch = weaker (66%). 3rd+ touch = weak/unreliable. Favor untouched nodes
Rate of Change: Rapid add = strong magnet | Rapid unwind = level weakens | Fast shifts = spikes/reversals
More tests = less bounce power. Watch speed of exposure change
Correct Hierarchy & Node Priority▼
Correct Hierarchy — NEVER break this: 1. Price Action → must agree 2. Heatseeker Context → validates 3. Risk:Reward → decides
If ANY layer fails = no trade
Node Priority (Gravity Model): 1. King Node → largest absolute value 2. Gatekeeper Node → deflection zone 3. Cluster/stacks → pin/chop zones 4. Single isolated nodes → lowest priority
Midpoints = MM trap zone (worst R:R) — middle of range = uncertainty. Best trade = fade edges, not midpoint
King Node (Daily Anchor) & Gatekeepers▼
King Node = largest node on map. Dealers prefer price near here by EOD/EOW. Treat as primary destination
Early-day touch → reject. Late-day approach → pin
Gatekeeper Nodes: sit between price & King Node. Test + reject → bias shift. Early rejection = high-prob reversal
If SPX, SPY, QQQ don't agree at Gatekeeper → stand aside
Bigger absolute value = stronger magnet. Mark only major nodes
Range, Trade Locations & Daily Routine▼
Upper extreme = repeated rejection zone. Lower extreme = repeated bounce zone
Trade plan: fade extremes · avoid midpoints · edge = edges
Best Trade Locations: Range edges · King Node area/approach late day · Gatekeeper rejection
NOT: midpoint · chasing breakouts into empty space · inside chop · chasing moves
Simple Daily Routine: 1. Mark King Node 2. Mark upper/lower range 3. Mark nearest gatekeepers 4. Wait for price to reach edge 5. Let price confirm → enter 6. Target next major node

What Is a Premium Chart?▼
Premium chart = price chart of an options CONTRACT — shows how the COST (premium) of that option moves over time
You are trading the premium price itself, not just the stock. Even if the stock moves correctly, the option's premium may not
Premium moves depending on: Time decay (theta) · Volatility changes (IV) · Momentum changes · Market sentiment around that strike/expiration
Reading the premium chart helps you understand where BUYERS & SELLERS are comfortable pricing the contract itself
Why Premium Charts Matter▼
You're trading premiums, not just the stock — premium charts show where the option itself is strong or weak so you don't get trapped buying high
Helps avoid buying at the 'top' of a move — if you buy when premium is near resistance you're likely overpaying & more likely to see an instant pullback
Reduces emotional trading — when you know where contracts historically gain or lose value, decisions become logical rather than emotional
Resistance exists: premium often rejects at known resistance. Exiting there protects gains & avoids being caught in reversals
Support entries = better Risk to Reward — when you enter near support, your downside is limited & even a small bounce can make you profitable
Core Concepts — Structure, Support & Resistance▼
Structure of chart: X-axis = time. Y-axis = premium price
Resistance Level: identify levels where premium repeatedly tops out (ex: $1.50 → $2.70). These are ideal EXIT zones for taking profit
Support Levels: identify flat or bounced areas where premium repeatedly holds (ex: $0.80 → $1)
Confirmation: premium often hits support when the underlying stock hits technical support — look for confluence between BOTH charts
★ Mark your charts ahead of time w/ horizontal lines for key levels. Always mark at least 2-3 levels & gaps
